SIIM26: Field Notes from Pittsburgh

What the imaging-informatics community is actually building, deploying, and worrying about

Society for Imaging Informatics in Medicine, Annual Meeting · David L. Lawrence Convention Center · A 3R Swiss Imaging Network field report from the team at SIIM: Benoît Rizk, Amine Korchi & Sergey Morozov. Sources: on-site session notes, the 3R SIIM poster, the EuSoMII European session, and public reporting (June 2026).

The one-line takeaway

The story of SIIM26 was not a smarter detector. It was the report. The field has largely solved “does the model work,” and moved to two harder questions: how do you deliver AI inside the workflow so the time it saves is real, and how do you watch it once it is live on real patients. Reporting platforms, agentic architectures, and post-deployment monitoring dominated; governance and reimbursement finally caught up to the technology.

Five threads: detection → reportingthe monitoring gapharness > modelopen-source & local LLMsEurope: gridlock vs. a Swiss playbook

1 · The headline shift: from detection to reporting

For a decade the imaging-AI narrative was detection: nodules, bleeds, fractures, emboli. At SIIM26 the announcements that mattered were about generating the report itself, the radiologist’s actual unit of work.

DeepHealth (RadNet): Reporting Pro

Speech recognition, AI findings & measurements, AI impressions, QA and structured reporting in one workflow. Live across RadNet at scale; US + UK.

Rad AI × Yale New Haven Health

Systemwide deal, Rad AI’s first major academic health system. 5 hospitals + 16 outpatient centres, 700,000+ exams/yr (Omni Impressions).

Harrison.ai: Harrison.rad.1.5

Drafts reports from images, priors and context. The only evaluated model to pass the FRCR 2B Short Case (median ~86.5 vs ~73.2 cut-off) where GPT-5, Claude and Gemini did not.

Radiology Partners: Mosaic

Builds the report in real time while the radiologist reads.

So the models work; that is no longer the interesting question. The interesting part is what happens after go-live.

2 · The deployment paradox & the monitoring gap

Two speakers who were not selling anything framed the central tension of the meeting.

The paradox: Eric Topol. Imaging AI is validated by dozens of randomised trials and barely deployed, while ~70% of US physicians already use language models that were never proven for clinical use. We are cautious where we have evidence, and casual where we do not.
The other half: Curt Langlotz. About three-quarters of FDA-cleared AI devices are radiology tools, and most run with no serious post-deployment monitoring. We clear models well, wrap them in workflow increasingly well, and still watch them poorly once live.

The bottleneck is the unglamorous layer: quality management, drift detection, observability. It does not demo well at a booth, but it is the difference between an AI that helps and an expensive screensaver whose result arrives after the report is signed. A reporting platform is only as trustworthy as the monitoring underneath it.

The governance scaffolding is catching up: the first-ever ACR-SIIM Practice Parameter for Imaging AI (adopted May 2026) covers selection, predeployment evaluation, ongoing monitoring and privacy; and the ACR Assess-AI quality registry. Over 18 months: 11 challenges, 500+ evaluations, 300+ radiologists, 40+ institutions, reported the best chest-X-ray model at ~88% concordance vs radiologists, up from 30-40% in 2024. The gap is now in oversight, not raw accuracy.

3 · Governance, economics & liability

  • The reimbursement trap. CMS’s efficiency adjustment means public “AI makes us faster” claims can invite further RVU cuts. The ACR Appendix S framing (Assistive, Augmentative, Autonomous) matters: “augmentative” protects reimbursement; “efficiency” language signals removable work.
  • Regulatory posture. The FDA denied a citizen petition (April 2026): prior clearance ≠ future competence across new indications. Its preferred update mechanism is the PCCP (Predetermined Change Control Plan). A Senate bill proposes a Medicare pathway for algorithm-based services with multi-year payment stability.
  • Liability signal. A jury-simulation study suggested reviewing AI separately (after your own read) drew fewer plaintiff verdicts than simultaneous review. That hints today’s default display workflow may carry hidden medico-legal risk. Track, don’t yet change.
  • Operating model. The “frontier practice” redesigns its operating model around AI and redeploys freed capacity, rather than bolting AI on as an error-checker.

4 · Agentic AI & the infrastructure layer

  • Workflow vs. agent. A workflow runs human-determined steps; an agent is a model + tools in a loop pursuing a goal. The decisive differentiator is the harness (tools, context, guardrails), not the model, since GPT-5, Claude Opus and Gemini are increasingly interchangeable.
  • MCP is becoming the standard. The Model Context Protocol is emerging as the common language for agent-to-tool communication. New procurement question: “Do you expose an MCP?” A Yale prototype draft-report agent pulls priors via a PACS tool, runs a foundation model for findings, then formats to each radiologist’s taste.
  • Microsoft healthcare-AI lab. Hands-on with MedImageInsight (one embedding backbone across X-ray/CT/MRI/US/fundus, zero-shot CLIP-style classification) and CXRReportGen (structured chest-X-ray findings with current+prior images and context, sub-second inference; Mayo fine-tuned further). Both ship as open-source in Azure AI Foundry. CT report generation is not yet solved. “Maybe by end of year.”
Concrete 3R pilot: fine-tune an open chest-X-ray report model (e.g., CXRReportGen) on ~1,000-2,000 retrospective local studies, likely IRB-exempt. Low cost, fits the AI-native delivery thesis from our poster.

5 · Open-source & local AI, driven by privacy

  • Local LLM stack. Ollama + Open WebUI (self-hosted, data stays on your network) for PHI-safe use; LM Studio for local runs on Apple Silicon. Cloud consumer endpoints log IPs and retain context, unsuitable for patient data; GDPR/Swiss-compliant providers (e.g., Mistral) are acceptable.
  • Evidence for radiology. A peer-reviewed study (Eur J Radiol AI) tested open-source models for PHI removal from reports; Qwen variants led, one reaching 100% PHI removal while preserving clinical content, support for a local-first de-identification pipeline.
  • Research hygiene. “Mutable vs. immutable surface”: lock the preprocessing, metric and test set; let the model iterate only on training scripts/hyperparameters. For literature work, feed tools like NotebookLM only cited references (not unpublished manuscripts) and verify citations against PubMed/Scholar by hand.

6 · The European lens, and 3R on the podium

The EuSoMII European session was the most useful reality check on adoption and regulation, and it was a 3R-led session. Amine Korchi (MD) and Benoît Rizk (MD) presented lessons from the 3R Swiss multi-center AI deployment; Merel Huisman (MD PhD, Radboudumc; EuSoMII board member) gave a complementary talk on the European AI market and regulatory landscape. Martin Willemink, Woojin Kim and Nina Kottler were among the attendees.

  • Adoption, real but uneven. ~50% of European radiologists use ≥1 AI tool (France ~80%, parts of NE Europe ~20%). ~446 AI solutions cleared in the US; Europe similar on paper but concentrated, only a few dozen clinically active.
  • Pricing under pressure. Per-study to large annual subscriptions, with growing downward price pressure as buyers consolidate.
  • Regulatory gridlock. The EU AI Act makes diagnostic AI high-risk; its overlap with MDR is unresolved and freezing some vendors. EHDS mandates full EU health-data interoperability by 2030.
  • The 3R playbook (Switzerland). The 3R talk (Korchi and Rizk) distilled 20 centres, 60+ radiologists, 5+ years: ~one new AI app onboarded per month via a standard process; monitor latency, late-rate and NPS; drop tools whose NPS doesn’t improve in ~6 months; ROI framed as modelled FTE capacity vs volume. The most transferable governance model in the session.

7 · The 3R contribution: a real-world study

The 3R team brought a poster that speaks straight to the meeting’s core theme: turning AI time-savings into real, redeployable capacity, measured the way a department actually experiences it.

“Impact of AI Tools on Radiologist Performance in Trauma X-ray Interpretation: A Multi-Center Real-World Study.” Novarina O, Morozov S, Heracleous N, Rizk B, Dufour B, Thouly C. 3R Swiss Imaging Network, Sion, Switzerland.

Why it landed

  • Operational metric, not reading time. 3R reports full operational report turnaround (minutes), not reading time (seconds), the gap the literature leaves open, and the kind of post-market metric the EU AI Act will want.
  • Delivery beats the algorithm. ~69% of AI delay was data routing, not inference; 7.2% of results arrived after sign-off. Auto-push at reconstruction, modality-stratified queues, and context-gating via the RIS referral indication are what convert a good model into felt time-savings.
  • Context-blindness & the Jevons paradox. With no referral context the AI processes every radiograph; 26.7% of doubt alerts fired on non-trauma (PPV 0.4%). Gating by indication (≥37% out-of-scope) cuts noise, doubts and cost. Yet only ~31% of radiologists reported a productivity benefit despite measured gains, capacity is reabsorbed unless deliberately redeployed.
  • Perceived value. Radiologists value AI first as a safety belt (71.1%), then as time gain (56.6%). The business lever: redeploy freed trauma-X-ray capacity to higher-acuity CT and MRI.

8 · What this means for us

MoveWhy
Buy delivery, not just accuracyScore vendors on auto-push, queue logic, latency, context-gating, and whether they expose an MCP, not only AUC.
Stand up monitoring before scalingAdopt ACR-SIIM parameter language; track latency, late-rate, concordance, NPS; keep the 3R “drop if NPS doesn’t move in 6 months” discipline.
Redeploy, don’t just saveDecide in advance where freed capacity goes (CT/MRI) or the Jevons paradox eats the gain.
Go local-first for sensitive workPilot Ollama/Open WebUI or LM Studio for de-identification and drafting; fine-tune an open CXR report model on local data.
Mind the framingSay “augmentative,” not “efficiency,” in anything public, which protects reimbursement and reduces liability.
